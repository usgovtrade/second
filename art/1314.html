"I grew up in a time and place where there were very few musical outlets for me to digest. I was also a big reader, a comic book collector, an old pulp fiction fanatic, and especially I loved watching old movie rarities, and old Saturday morning cartoons. And as I read more and more fiction from all different genres, I've come to love the language of music as much as any other literature out there. "As a musician myself, I have grown to love the language of music as much as I love the words, and when it comes to songwriting, it's not just words, but the way those words sound coming out of your speakers that creates all the moods and the emotions that are meant to be created. "As I got older and grew into my own, I got to see the words to 'In Bloom' on the back of the Nirvana Nevermind album, and they really stood out to me as the perfect song to say a whole lot in such a small amount of time. As I became a more complex person in my mid-teens, so did my interest in Nirvana's music; it was so dark and it just represented something pure, and very real, and honest. It was music of the people, with no bullshit. The name Nirvana was taken from the D. H. Lawrence novel Lady Chatterley's Lover (1893), which concerns an affair between a young English noblewoman and an aristocrat's gamekeeper; Kurt Cobain wanted a name that suggested "something from a different culture... something with an interesting history," as he explained in a 1992 interview with Mojo. For years, Nirvana's career was one of great ups and downs. During its formative years, Nirvana was signed to DGC Records—the Geffen imprint of the major label giant Geffen Records—for which the band recorded the first of three landmark albums with legendary producer Butch Vig, Bleach (1989), Nevermind (1991), and In Utero (1993).
